\begin{abstract}

\textbf{Background}:
  Selecting an optimal portfolio allocation across a universe of assets is a central problem in 
  investment management. A wide range of allocation strategies have been proposed, from naive              
  diversification to theoretically grounded optimisation frameworks, yet their relative out-of-sample      
  performance remains contested, particularly across varying market regimes.  
  This study empirically compares four portfolio strategies to assess whether model sophistication translates 
  into superior investment performance. \\
\vspace{0.5mm}
\textbf{Methodology}:
  This study implements and compares four portfolio strategies: equal weight, Markowitz mean-variance, 
  Black-Litterman and Risk Parity applied to an 18-asset UK multi-asset portfolio comprising 15 FTSE 100 equities, 
  a UK government bond ETF, a gold ETC and a broad commodities ETF, over the period January 2015 to December 2025. 
  Strategies are evaluated using a monthly rebalancing framework and assessed across annualised return, volatility, 
  Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown and portfolio turnover. \\
\vspace{0.5mm}
  \textbf{Results}:
  We find results broadly consistent with DeMiguel et al. (2009), with the naive benchmark 
  proving difficult to displace on a risk-adjusted basis over the full sample period. The optimisation 
  strategies exhibit meaningfully different risk profiles, however, suggesting that Sharpe ratio alone does not 
  fully capture the practical trade-offs between approaches. \\
\end{abstract}

\end{frontmatter}

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The equal-weight portfolio is robust because it does not rely on 
  estimated parameters and is therefore unaffected by estimation error.
  \item The Efficient Frontier is the set of portfolios that deliver the minimum 
  possible variance for each level of expected return.
  \item Empirical evidence suggests that many optimisation models fail to 
  consistently outperform the equal-weight portfolio out of sample.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{The Portfolio Allocation Problem}
\indent \indent The portfolio allocation problem is the fundamental question in investment management. 
It asks: How should capital be distributed across available investment opportunities to best achieve 
an investor's objectives? \\
\indent The problem was intractable until 1952, when Harry Markowitz published \textit{Portfolio Selection} in the 
\textit{Journal of Finance} and turned it into a clean optimisation problem, and the “efficient frontier” 
gave investors a principled answer. Markowitz's mean variance model minimises portfolio variance for a given 
level of expected return. It was revolutionary in the consideration of variance as the risk, standing as a 
hallmark of modern portfolio theory. In practice however, the model has proven difficult to implement reliably. \\
\indent Michaud (1989) challenged this view directly, arguing that small estimation errors in expected 
returns produce wildly unstable portfolio recommendations. \\
\indent In 2009, DeMiguel et al. delivered a provocative result: he found that even sophisticated strategies 
incorporating shrinkage estimators and Bayesian methods failed to consistently outperform 1/N out-of-sample, 
suggesting that estimation error dominates theoretical optimality for typical portfolio problems. This tension 
between the theoretical elegance of optimisation and its empirical fragility motivates the present study. 


\subsection{Research Objectives}
\indent \indent This study addresses three main questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Do optimisation models outperform a naive 1/N benchmark
    out-of-sample, applied to a multi-asset UK portfolio over 
    a ten-year period? 
    \item Which model offers the best risk-adjusted performance, 
    as measured by the Sharpe ratio?
    \item How does model performance vary across distinct market
    regimes: the Brexit referendum (2016), the COVID-19 crash (2020),
    and the 2022 rate-hiking cycle?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The equal weight portfolio}

\indent \indent The equal-weight (1/N) portfolio assigns an identical weight of 1/N to each asset in the portfolio, holding N assets, regardless of any asset-specific 
characteristics such as return, volatility, or correlation. 
\citet{demiguel_optimal_2009} tested 14 optimisation models across 7 datasets. None consistently outperformed the naive equal-weight portfolio: 
\begin{equation}
  w_i = \frac{1}{N} \quad \text{for} \quad 1 \leq i \leq N
\end{equation}
\indent But why? When expected returns are estimated from historical data in order to determine optimal portfolio weights, the estimates 
improve as more data is collected, but they do so slowly. \newline
Formally, the estimation error decays as
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mu} - \mu = \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the number of observations.

Because the error shrinks at rate $\frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$, halving the 
error requires four times as much data, meaning that with monthly data 
and realistic return distributions, approximately 500 years of observations 
are required for statistically reliable estimates, especially since these
errors are amplified through the term $\Sigma^{-1}\hat{\mu}$. \newline
\indent The equal-weight portfolio sidesteps this problem entirely: by assigning a fixed weight to each asset, it requires no parameter estimation 
and is therefore immune to estimation error by construction. \newline
The cost of this simplicity, however, is that the portfolio ignores all information about assets' risk and return characteristics, makes 
no attempt to diversify risk efficiently and cannot adapt to changing market conditions.

\subsection{Markowitz's mean-variance optimisation}
\label{sec:mvo}

\indent \indent \citet{markowitz_portfolio_1952} formulated \textit{Portfolio Selection} in 1952, approaching the asset 
allocation problem as a constrained optimisation problem: minimise 
portfolio variance for a given level of expected return, the foundation of the mean-variance model.
His work stands as a hallmark of modern portfolio theory.\newline
\indent Formally, the problem states:
\begin{equation}                                         
  \begin{aligned}
  \min_{\mathbf{w}} \hat{\sigma} =
  \mathbf{w}^\top \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\ \mathbf{w}
  \quad \text{s.t.} \quad
  \mathbf{w}^\top \boldsymbol{\mu} = \mu_p
  \quad \text{;} \quad 
  \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{1} = 1
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

with $\mu_p$ as the scalar target portfolio return.

The first constraint, also known as the return constraint, fixes the portfolio to a 
specific point at the target level $\mu_p$, pinning the solution to a specific point on the "Efficient Frontier".
By varying $\mu_p$ across all feasible values, the complete set of minimum-variance 
portfolios - the Efficient Frontier - is traced out.
The second constraint, i.e. the budget constraint, ensures the weights represent a valid
allocation of wealth, hence that the sum of all weights is always equal to $1$. \newline
\indent It is important to note that no domain-restriction condition is imposed on the budget constraint,
each $w_i$ may take any real value, in particular, negative ones.
Practically speaking, a negative weight corresponds to a short position in the associated asset.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{../images/plots/theory/efficient_frontier.pdf}
    \caption{Efficient Frontier estimated using sample covariance}
    \label{fig:efficient_frontier}
\end{figure}

Because the objective function is quadratic and the constraints are linear equalities, we cannot solve the problem by direct substitution 
(two constraints, n unknowns). We therefore use the method of Lagrange multipliers. 
The Lagrangian formulation is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}, \lambda, \gamma)
      = \mathbf{w}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}
      - \lambda\!\left(\mathbf{w}^\top \boldsymbol{\mu} - \mu_p\right)
      - \gamma\!\left(\mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{w} - 1\right)
\end{equation}

where $\lambda$ and $\gamma$ are the Lagrange multipliers associated with the return and budget constraints, respectively. 
Intuitively, $\lambda$ captures the marginal cost of requiring a higher return (i.e. how much additional variance must be accepted 
per unit increase in $\mu_p$), and $\gamma$ enforces full investment.

Taking the first-order condition:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}} = 2\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w} - \lambda \boldsymbol{\mu} - \gamma \mathbf{1} = \mathbf{0}
\end{equation}

Solving gives:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}_p = \frac{\lambda}{2}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu} + \frac{\gamma}{2}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}
\end{equation}

Imposing the budget and return constraints leads to the following two-fund theorem: all efficient portfolios can be expressed as a 
linear combination of any two distinct efficient portfolios.
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}_A = \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}
  \qquad
  \mathbf{w}_B = \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\mu}}
\end{equation}

Note that $\mathbf{w}_A$ is the \emph{global minimum variance} (GMV) portfolio, 
the leftmost point on the efficient frontier, obtained by dropping the 
return constraint and minimising variance subject only to full investment:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{GMV}}
    = \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}{\mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\mathbf{1}}
\end{equation}

And the \emph{maximum Sharpe ratio} portfolio is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{MSR}}
    = \frac{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\mu} - r_f \mathbf{1})}
           {\mathbf{1}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\mu} - r_f \mathbf{1})}
  \label{eq:msr}
\end{equation}
\indent Despite its theoretical elegance, Markowitz optimisation suffers from a well-documented problem. 
As mentioned above, the solution depends critically on $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, both estimated from historical data. \newline
\indent Small errors in $\mathbf{\mu}$ can cause large shifts in optimal weights.
\citet{michaud_markowitz_1989} characterised the optimiser as an "error maximiser": it systematically overweights assets with 
overestimated returns and underestimated variances.

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Black-Litterman uses market-cap weights as a neutral 
  starting point for expected returns.
  \item Subjective investor views are blended with the equilibrium
  prior through a Bayesian update.
  \item The optimisation step in Black-Litterman is the same as MVO
  with $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{BL}$ replacing the sample mean.                                                    
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\subsection{Black-Litterman and its Bayesian approach}

\indent \indent The Black-Litterman (BL) model was developed by Fischer Black and 
Robert Litterman at Goldman Sachs in the early 1990s 
to address the instability of Mean-Variance Optimisation \citep{black_asset_1991, black_global_1992}.

\indent The BL model addresses two shortcomings of classic mean-variance optimisation:
\begin{enumerate}
  \vspace{-2mm}
  \item What expected returns should be used? \newline
  Rather than relying on historical data, BL uses returns implied by current 
  market-cap weights as the neutral starting point.
  \item How should the views be incorporated? \newline
  BL offers a formal Bayesian framework for blending subjective investor views 
  with this equilibrium prior, weighting each source inversely by its uncertainty.
  \vspace{-2mm}
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Step 1. Equilibrium Returns}} \\
\indent The model takes as its starting point the equilibrium expected returns implied 
by the market portfolio, i.e. the portfolio where each asset is held in proportion
to its market capitalisation.
Assuming the market portfolio is mean-variance efficient, the equilibrium excess returns are:
\begin{equation}
\Pi = \delta \Sigma w_{m}
\end{equation}
where $w_m$ is the market-cap weight vector, $\Sigma$ is the covariance 
matrix of asset returns and $\delta$ is the risk-aversion coefficient
\begin{equation}
  \delta = \frac{r_m - r_f}{\sigma_m^2}
\end{equation}
with $r_m$ the return of the market portfolio and $\sigma^2$ its variance. \\

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Step 2. Express Views}} \\
\indent A view can be absolute or relative, for example "asset A will return 5\%" or 
"asset A will outperform asset B by 2\%".
Each view has two components: the expected return itself and a confidence 
level attached to it. \newline
\indent Formally, k views are expressed through the following system:
\begin{equation}
  P \boldsymbol{\mu} = \boldsymbol{Q} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}
  \qquad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Omega)
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l l}
\toprule
\textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
$P \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ & \emph{Pick matrix}: encodes which assets views involve \\
$\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^k$ & Vector of expected returns \\
$\boldsymbol{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^k$ & Expected view returns \\
$\Omega \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$ & View uncertainty matrix (diagonal) \\
$\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Omega)$ & View error: deviation of true returns from stated views \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\indent Assets are ranked by their return over the previous 12 months, excluding the most 
recent month to eliminate short-term reversal effects. The highest-return tercile forms
the winner portfolio, while the lowest-return decile constitutes the loser portfolio. \\
\indent The view is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{p}_{k}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\mu} = q_k
\end{equation}
\indent $\boldsymbol{p}_k$ assigns equal positive weights to winner assets and equal negative weights 
to loser assets, forming a long-short momentum view. The expected return 
spread $q_k$ is estimated over a rolling window at each rebalancing date as asset rankings change. \\

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Step 3. Bayesian Update}} \\
\indent The prior on expected returns is:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\Pi}, \tau \boldsymbol{\Sigma})
\end{equation}
\indent This encodes the belief that expected returns are centred on the equilibrium 
vector $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$, with uncertainty scaled by $\tau$. Combining this prior 
with the view system via Bayes' theorem yields the posterior distribution over 
expected returns:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu} \mid \boldsymbol{Q} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu_{BL}}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma_{BL}})
\end{equation}
where the posterior mean is:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu_{BL}} = 
    \left[(\tau \Sigma)^{-1} 
    + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}P\right]^{-1}\left[(\tau \Sigma)^{-1}\boldsymbol{\Pi} 
    + P^{\top}\Omega^{-1}\boldsymbol{Q}\right]
\end{equation}
\indent The posterior mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{BL}$ is a precision-weighted average of the 
equilibrium prior $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ and the investor's views $\boldsymbol{Q}$, with each 
source weighted inversely by its uncertainty. 
When views are diffuse (large $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$), $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{BL}$ 
remains close to $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$. 
If the views are held with high confidence (small $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$), 
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{BL}$ tilts toward $\mathbf{Q}$. \newline

This blending property is central to the appeal of the BL framework: 
Rather than overriding the equilibrium prior, views are incorporated gradually 
and in proportion to their confidence. This acts as a natural regulariser, because the 
prior pulls weights toward the market portfolio, the model is far less prone to the 
concentrated, unstable allocations that plague classical MVO. \\

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Step 4. Portfolio Optimisation}} \\
\indent The optimal portfolio is obtained by applying the same Sharpe-maximising 
objective derived in Section~\ref{sec:mvo} (equation~\ref{eq:msr}), 
substituting $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\text{BL}}$ in place of the sample mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$.

\subsection{Risk Parity}

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Risk Parity allocates capital such that every asset contributes                                       
    equally to total portfolio volatility, not equally to capital.
  \item Unlike MVO and Black-Litterman, Risk Parity requires no estimate
    of expected returns: allocations depend only on the covariance structure.
  \item The equal risk contribution condition $w_i(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})_i = c$
    for all $i$ has no closed-form solution and in general and is solved numerically.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\indent \indent The modern Risk Parity framework was popularised by Ray Dalio 
at Bridgewater Associates and underpins the All Weather strategy introduced 
in the 1990s. The approach was subsequently
formalised by \citet{maillard_properties_2010}, who derived the mathematical
conditions for equal risk contribution portfolios. \\
\indent The central motivation
departs entirely from the Mean Variance and Black-Litterman frameworks: rather than optimising
expected return for a given level of risk, Risk Parity ignores return estimates
altogether and instead focuses on how risk is distributed across the portfolio.

\indent Risk Parity addresses a structural limitation shared by both MVO and
the equal-weight portfolio:
\begin{enumerate}
  \vspace{-2mm}
  \item In MVO and BL, allocations depend on estimated expected returns, which
  are noisy and prone to estimation error.
  \item In equal-weight portfolios, capital is diversified equally but risk is not.
  Assets with high volatility dominate total portfolio risk despite receiving the
  same capital allocation.
  \vspace{-2mm}
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Risk Decomposition}} \\
\indent Portfolio volatility $\sigma_p = \sqrt{\mathbf{w}^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w}}$
is a homogeneous function of degree 1 in $\mathbf{w}$. By Euler's homogeneous
function theorem, it admits the following decomposition:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_p = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \frac{\partial \sigma_p}{\partial w_i}
\end{equation}
The \emph{marginal risk contribution} (MRC) of asset $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{MRC}_i = \frac{\partial \sigma_p}{\partial w_i}
  = \frac{(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})_i}{\sigma_p}
\end{equation}
and the total \emph{risk contribution} (RC) of asset $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{RC}_i = w_i \cdot \text{MRC}_i
  = \frac{w_i\,(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})_i}{\sigma_p}
\end{equation}
with the useful property that $\sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{RC}_i = \sigma_p$. \\

\noindent \textbf{\emph{The Risk Parity Condition}} \\
\indent The portfolio is said to be \emph{risk-balanced} when every asset
contributes equally to total portfolio volatility:
\begin{equation}
  \text{RC}_i = \frac{\sigma_p}{N} \quad \forall\, i
\end{equation}
Since $\sigma_p$ appears on both sides, this reduces to the equivalent condition:
\begin{equation}
  w_i\,(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w})_i = c \quad \forall\, i
  \label{eq:rp}
\end{equation}
for some constant $c > 0$, subject to $\mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{w} = 1$ and $\mathbf{w} \geq \mathbf{0}$.
This system has no closed-form solution in general and is solved numerically;
the implementation details are described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. \\

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{../images/plots/theory/risk_parity_illustration.pdf}
    \caption{Risk parity weights as a function of asset variance}
    \label{fig:risk_parity_illustration}
\end{figure}

\noindent \textbf{\emph{Special Case: Uncorrelated Assets}} \\
\indent When $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is diagonal, the risk parity condition reduces to
an analytic solution. Equal risk contribution then requires each asset's weight to be
proportional to the inverse of its volatility:
\begin{equation}
  w_i^{\mathrm{RP}} = \frac{1/\sigma_i}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{N} 1/\sigma_j}
\end{equation}
\indent This inverse-volatility weighting is the closed-form limit of risk parity, and also
serves as the natural initialisation point for the numerical solver in the general case.

\indent Despite its appeal, Risk Parity has a well-known structural consequence:
it mechanically overweights low-volatility assets. In a portfolio containing
both equities and government bonds, bonds receive substantially larger
capital allocations precisely because their volatility is lower, which raises
the question of whether the resulting portfolio is truly diversified in an
economic sense or merely in a statistical one.

\subsection{Model Implementation}
\label{sec:model-implementation}
\indent \indent All models are implemented from first principles in Python using
\texttt{NumPy} and \texttt{Pandas}, following an object-oriented design in which
each strategy inherits from a common abstract base class. This ensures full
transparency and reproducibility of all optimisation steps. The complete source
code is publicly available at the project repository \citep{scr_repo}. \\
\indent All models share a rolling estimation framework: at each monthly rebalancing
date, covariance and return inputs are re-estimated using the preceding 252
trading days of data. Table~\ref{tab:implementation} summarises the key
implementation parameters for each strategy.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Implementation parameters by strategy}
\begin{tabular}{l l l l}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Window} & \textbf{Constraints} & \textbf{Solver} \\
\midrule

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\label{tab:implementation}
\end{table}

\indent Equal Weight assigns $w_i = 1/N$ at each rebalancing date 
and requires no parameter estimation. \\

\indent In the Mean-Variance model, the maximum Sharpe ratio portfolio is solved 
via an active-set method: assets with negative target weights are iteratively pruned from the
investment set and the system is re-solved until all remaining weights satisfy
the long-only constraint $w_i \geq 0$. A small ridge term $\lambda = 10^{-8}$
is added to the diagonal of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ at each step to
guarantee numerical positive definiteness. \\

\indent Risk Parity solves the equal risk contribution system
(equation~\ref{eq:rp}) via Newton-Raphson iteration, initialised with
inverse-volatility weights. Convergence is typically achieved within 10
iterations. \\

\indent The Black-Litterman model requires two calibration parameters.
The \textbf{prior uncertainty scalar} $\tau \in (0,1)$ controls how much uncertainty
surrounds the equilibrium prior: a small $\tau$ implies strong confidence that expected
returns are close to $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$, assigning little weight to the views. We set
$\tau = 0.05$, consistent with the original implementation of
\citet{he_intuition_2002}, who argued that uncertainty about the prior should be
considerably smaller than uncertainty about the returns themselves. \newline 
\indent The \textbf{risk-aversion coefficient} $\delta$ determines the magnitude of the
equilibrium returns via $\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \delta \boldsymbol{\Sigma}
\mathbf{w}_m$; a higher $\delta$ implies investors require greater compensation per unit
of portfolio variance, producing larger implied equilibrium returns. We set $\delta = 3$,
a standard value in the BL literature \citep{walters_black-litterman_2011}. A sensitivity
analysis varying both parameters across a grid is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:practical-implications}. \newline
\indent The investor views incorporated are motivated by the momentum effect documented by
\citet{jegadeesh_returns_1993}, showing that stocks with high returns over the prior 
3-12 months continue to outperform stocks with low prior returns over the subsequent 
3-12 months, generating abnormal returns of approximately 1\% per month. 
This persistent pattern offers an empirically grounded basis for constructing views 
within the BL framework. \newline 
\indent A long-short view is then formed between the top and
bottom terciles. The posterior expected returns $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{BL}$ are passed
into the same active-set MVO solver used for the standard MVO strategy.

\subsection{Backtesting Framework}
\indent \indent The four strategies are evaluated using a walk-forward backtesting 
engine to ensure zero look-ahead bias. The engine enforces a strict information 
barrier by passing only prices up to the rebalancing date; the covariance dependent 
strategies (MVO, BL, RP) then estimate over a 252 trading day sub-window. 
This distinction is already implicit in Table~\ref{tab:implementation}
(Section \ref{sec:model-implementation}). \\

\indent A starting capital of \pounds 100{,}000 is deployed on the first available 
trading day. The portfolio is thereafter rebalanced on the final trading day of each
calendar month, with all positions revalued daily at the adjusted closing price. \\

\indent The simulation assumes perfect asset divisibility (i.e. the existence of fractional
shares) and immediate execution at the closing price on each rebalancing date. Transaction
costs are not modelled; portfolio turnover is instead reported as a standalone metric, allowing
the reader to assess the practical trading burden of each strategy independently.
\indent Bid-ask spreads and market impact are not accounted for; given that
all 18 assets are large-cap and exchange-traded, it is reasonable to assume
sufficient liquidity to absorb the portfolio's order flow without material
execution slippage. \\

\indent Each simulation run produces a \texttt{BacktestResult} object containing the daily 
equity curve, full position history and a trade log. Performance metrics are computed 
from the equity curve at the conclusion of each run and are defined in 
Section~\ref{sec:performance-metrics}.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
\label{sec:performance-metrics}

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Sharpe and Sortino use annualised arithmetic mean return $\mu_p$, not CAGR, in the numerator.
  \item Maximum drawdown measures the largest peak-to-trough loss in portfolio value over the sample.
  \item Average monthly turnover quantifies rebalancing intensity and serves as a proxy for implementation cost.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\indent \indent Nine metrics are used to evaluate each strategy, grouped
into three categories: return, risk, and risk-adjusted. Let
$r_t = V_t / V_{t-1} - 1$ denote the daily portfolio return,
$V_0$ and $V_T$ the initial and terminal portfolio values, $T$ the total
number of trading days, $\mu_p = \bar{r}_t \times 252$ the annualised
arithmetic mean return, and $r_f$ the mean annualised risk-free rate over
the sample. Table~\ref{tab:metrics} defines each metric formally. \\

\indent Return metrics capture the magnitude of wealth creation. 
CAGR annualises the compounded total return to allow comparison across 
strategies regardless of sub-period variation. \newline 
\indent Risk metrics characterise the severity and shape of losses: 
maximum drawdown measures the worst peak-to-trough decline an investor 
would have experienced; VaR and CVaR characterise tail losses from the 
empirical return distribution without parametric assumptions. \newline 
\indent Risk-adjusted metrics normalise excess return by some measure of 
risk: the Sharpe ratio uses total volatility, while the Sortino ratio 
penalises only downside deviations, making it more appropriate when
return distributions are asymmetric.

\centering
\caption{Performance metrics used to evaluate each strategy}
\begin{tabular}{l l l}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Formula} & \textbf{Category} \\
\midrule
Total Return
    & $V_T / V_0 - 1$
    & Return \\
CAGR
    & $(V_T / V_0)^{252/T} - 1$
    & Return \\
Volatility
    & $\hat{\sigma}(r_t) \cdot \sqrt{252}$
    & Risk \\
Max Drawdown
    & $\displaystyle\min_t \left(\frac{V_t}{\max_{s \leq t} V_s} - 1\right)$
    & Risk \\

    & $\mathbb{E}[\,r_t \mid r_t \leq \text{VaR}\,]$

    & Risk-Adjusted \\

\end{tabular}

\end{table}

\indent VaR and CVaR are computed from the empirical distribution of daily
returns without a parametric assumption. $\hat{\sigma}_{\mathrm{down}}$
denotes the annualised standard deviation of negative daily returns only,
so the Sortino ratio penalises downside volatility exclusively. \newline
\indent Average monthly turnover measures the mean total absolute weight change
across all assets at each rebalancing date, providing a strategy-level
indicator of trading activity.

\subsection{Portfolio Characteristics}

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Allocation stability}} \\
\indent \indent The three optimised strategies differ
substantially in how they distribute and maintain their allocations over time. MVO is
the most unstable: concentration rotates between a small number of assets, most
notably IGLT.L and SGLD.L while the majority of the portfolio sits near zero for
extended stretches. This "all or nothing" behaviour is characteristic of
mean-variance optimisation, which tends to produce corner solutions that change
dramatically from one rebalancing date to the next. \newline 
\indent Black-Litterman is smoother,
with weights more evenly distributed and less violent rotation, though IGLT.L
consistently attracts a large allocation from 2018 onwards: a concentration risk
that partially undermines the diversification the model is designed to provide. \newline
\indent Risk Parity is the most stable: allocations are broadly uniform across assets for
most of the period, consistent with its objective of equalising risk contribution
rather than capital. The persistent overweight in IGLT.L reflects its low historical
volatility (~6\%), which mechanically attracts a larger risk budget within the framework. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Turnover and the cost of complexity}} \\
\indent \indent High turnover is not
a sign of a well-functioning strategy, it is a cost. Figure~\ref{fig:turnover}

and almost fourteen times Equal Weight (2.5\%). The chart also reveals that MVO's
turnover is not constant but spikes sharply at certain rebalancing dates, particularly
around regime shifts, reflecting its sensitivity to changes in the estimated return
and covariance inputs. \newline

fees and bid-ask spreads that erode net returns. Given that MVO already delivers the
weakest gross performance, its net returns after implementation costs would be weaker
still. Equal Weight, by contrast, requires minimal intervention yet outperforms all
three optimised strategies over the full period. This is perhaps the most pointed
finding of the section: \textbf{complexity does not necessarily pay.}

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Why did 1/N perform well?}

\indent \indent The headline finding of this study is that Equal Weight, a strategy
requiring no parameter estimation and no optimisation, delivered the highest cumulative
return, the highest Sharpe ratio and the highest Sortino ratio of all four strategies over
the full 2015-2025 period. This is not a marginal result: it outperformed the next-best
strategy (Black-Litterman) by over 31 percentage points in total return and by 0.1 Sharpe
units. \newline
\indent The natural question is: Why do three strategies that explicitly attempt to exploit
information about returns and covariances consistently fail to improve on a naive
benchmark? \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Estimation error as the root cause}} \\
\indent The core explanation is estimation error. Mean-variance optimisation and its
derivatives require two sets of inputs: a vector of expected returns $\boldsymbol{\mu}$
and a covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$. With 18 assets, this amounts to 18 mean
estimates and 171 distinct covariance entries (189 parameters in total) each estimated
from a rolling window of approximately 252 daily observations. \newline
\indent The statistical precision of
these estimates is inherently limited: sample means estimated from one year of data carry
enormous standard errors, and small perturbations in $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ translate into
large changes in the optimiser's output. \citet{michaud_markowitz_1989} characterised this as the
"error maximisation" problem: the optimiser treats estimation noise as genuine signal and
concentrates heavily into the assets whose returns happened to look most attractive over
the estimation window, regardless of whether that outperformance will persist. \newline
\indent Equal Weight
sidesteps this entirely by assuming no asset is expected to outperform any other:
a prior that, on short rolling windows, turns out to be approximately correct. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{An established empirical regularity}} \\
\indent This finding is not unique to our dataset. \citet{demiguel_optimal_2009}
conducted a systematic comparison of 14 portfolio optimisation strategies across seven
empirical datasets and found that none significantly outperformed 1/N on a risk-adjusted
basis out-of-sample. \newline 
\indent They derived a theoretical condition under which MVO improves on EW:
the estimation window must exceed $N(N+1)/2$ periods, which for 18 assets implies over
170 years of data; far beyond any realistic investment horizon. Our results replicate
their finding on a UK multi-asset universe with a ten-year sample period. The 1/N puzzle
is therefore not a curiosity of our particular dataset, but a well-documented property of
mean-variance optimisation in realistic finite-sample settings. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Why this universe is particularly amenable to EW}} \\
\indent The composition of the 18-asset portfolio matters. Because the universe spans
genuinely distinct risk classes: UK equities, government bonds, gold, and broad
commodities, a naive equal-weight allocation achieves meaningful cross-asset
diversification without requiring any return forecast. \newline
\indent Had the universe consisted of
18 highly correlated stocks within a single sector, the free lunch of naive diversification
would be substantially smaller, and the marginal value of optimisation would be greater.
\indent The multi-asset structure also means that EW's equity overweight relative to bonds
happened to pay off during the prolonged equity bull market of 2016-2021, a period
which accounts for the majority of the performance gap. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Implications for portfolio construction}} \\
\indent These results should not be read as a rejection of portfolio optimisation theory.
The Markowitz framework and its Bayesian extensions are theoretically coherent; the issue
is that their practical implementation with short estimation windows and no regularisation
produces unstable, concentrated portfolios that perform poorly out-of-sample. \newline
\indent Black-Litterman was designed precisely to address this by anchoring expected 
returns to a stable equilibrium prior and updating it only partially in the direction 
of investor views, thereby reducing sensitivity to noisy historical return estimates. 
BL's underperformance in this study reflects the specific calibration
choices made, in particular the dominance of momentum views over the equilibrium prior,
rather than a fundamental deficiency in the Bayesian approach.
This point is developed further in Section~\ref{sec:practical-implications}.

\subsection{The risk parity puzzle}
\label{sec:rp-puzzle}

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Risk Parity's structural overweight in low-volatility gilts drags returns during equity bull markets.
  \item The 2022 rate-hiking cycle broke the equity-bond diversification logic that underpins the strategy.
  \item Unlevered risk parity lacks the return compensation that leveraged institutional implementations achieve.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\indent \indent Risk Parity recorded the lowest volatility (10\%), the joint-smallest
maximum drawdown ($-23.7\%$), and the most stable allocation profile of any strategy in
this study. By the conventional logic of risk-adjusted performance, a portfolio that
controls downside more effectively should produce a competitive Sharpe ratio. \newline 
\indent Instead,
Risk Parity delivered the lowest Sharpe (0.19) and the lowest total return (41.2\%) of
the three optimised strategies, a result that warrants explanation. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Structural bond overweight}} \\
\indent The root cause lies in how risk parity assigns capital. Because the strategy
equalises risk contributions rather than weights, assets with low historical volatility
mechanically receive a disproportionately large allocation. IGLT.L, the UK gilts ETF,
carries approximately one-third the volatility of the equity constituents (6\% versus
18-25\%), and consequently attracts a far larger risk budget than any individual equity. \newline
\indent Over the 2015-2025 period, UK government bonds delivered modest nominal returns, and the
strategy was structurally underweight in the equities that drove the majority of cross-asset
returns. \newline
\indent This is not a calibration error or an implementation flaw but it is the defining
consequence of risk parity on a multi-asset universe in which equities dominate risk-adjusted
performance. The strategy achieves what it promises: equal risk contribution. The cost is a
persistent drag on returns during equity bull markets. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Correlation breakdown in the 2022 regime}} \\
\indent Risk parity's theoretical appeal rests on the historically low or negative
correlation between equities and government bonds. When equities fall, bonds typically
rally, providing ballast that stabilises the portfolio. \newline 
\indent In 2022, that relationship broke
sharply: aggressive central bank rate hikes drove both asset classes lower simultaneously,
producing a joint drawdown that is particularly damaging to a strategy holding a large
bond allocation. \newline 
\indent Risk Parity returned $-4.3\%$ over the 2022 rate-hiking cycle, worse
than Equal Weight ($+1.5\%$) despite carrying significantly less equity risk. The regime
that risk parity was, in principle, best positioned to weather proved to be one of its
worst episodes in the sample. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{The missing ingredient: Leverage}} \\
\indent Institutional implementations of risk parity, most notably Bridgewater's
All Weather strategy, apply leverage to scale the low-volatility portfolio up to a
target return level, compensating for the return drag from bond overweighting. \newline
\indent In an unlevered setting, the lower return profile is not offset, and the strategy's
risk-adjusted advantage over equities is structurally compressed. Our implementation
is unlevered throughout, which is the appropriate choice for a like-for-like comparison
with the other strategies, but it means Risk Parity is evaluated in a setting that
partially disadvantages its intended design. \newline 
\indent A leveraged version would likely produce a
materially different risk-return profile, though at the cost of introducing leverage risk
and financing costs beyond the scope of this study.

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Qualification}} \\
\indent These results should not be read as a dismissal of risk parity as a framework.
Risk Parity provided the best downside protection during the COVID-19 crash ($-13.7\%$
versus Equal Weight's $-25.8\%$) and consistently maintained lower peak-to-trough
drawdowns across the full sample. For investors with lower loss tolerance, a shorter
investment horizon, or a liability-matching objective, the drawdown characteristics may
justify the return sacrifice. The puzzle, more precisely stated, is not that Risk Parity
is a poor strategy in general, but that the specific conditions of 2015--2025 --- a
prolonged equity bull market followed by a simultaneous bond and equity drawdown in
2022 --- were precisely those least suited to an unlevered risk parity implementation
on a UK multi-asset universe.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
\label{sec:practical-implications}

\indent \indent The results of this study carry several practical lessons for
portfolio construction beyond the model-specific findings discussed above. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Model selection depends on investor objective}} \\
\indent No single strategy dominates across all dimensions of the performance space. A
practitioner's choice should be conditioned on their investment objective:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Total return / risk-adjusted performance}: Equal Weight. No strategy
    in this study improved on it over the full sample.
    \item \textit{Drawdown control / capital preservation}: Risk Parity. Its maximum
    drawdown of $-23.7\%$ and rapid recovery from most dips suit investors with lower
    loss tolerance or liability-matching objectives, provided they accept the structural
    return drag from bond overweighting.
    \item \textit{Systematic active tilts}: Black-Litterman, but only with carefully
    constructed views and well-calibrated parameters. A mechanically implemented
    momentum signal, as used here, does not reliably add value relative to the naive
    benchmark on this universe.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Turnover as a hidden performance drag}} \\
\indent Transaction costs are absent from the gross performance figures reported
throughout this study, but they are not absent from reality. MVO's average monthly
turnover of 34.7\% means that at every rebalancing, more than a third of the portfolio
is being traded. Even modest transaction costs would reduce MVO's already weakest
net returns further still, widening the gap with Equal Weight. \newline 
\indent For a retail investor
facing bid-ask spreads and brokerage fees that are multiples larger, MVO is effectively
unimplementable without explicit cost controls such as turnover constraints or a minimum
trade threshold. \newline 
\indent By contrast, Equal Weight's 2.5\% average monthly turnover imposes a
negligible implementation cost, reinforcing its practical appeal beyond its favourable
gross performance. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{BL parameter sensitivity: prior domination confirmed}} \\
\indent To assess the robustness of the Black-Litterman results to calibration choices,
we ran a grid search over $\tau \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.25\}$ and $\delta \in
\{1, 2, 3, 5\}$, yielding 16 independent backtests. The results are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:bl_sensitivity}. The finding is striking: Returns and Sharpe ratio are
\textbf{completely insensitive to $\tau$} across the full range tested, for any
fixed $\delta$, returns vary less than 0.01 across all four values of $\tau$.
This is direct empirical evidence of prior domination: the momentum views $Q$ are
sufficiently large relative to the equilibrium prior $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ that varying
$\tau$, which controls how much weight the prior receives, has no measurable
effect. \newline 
\indent The posterior is determined almost entirely by the views, regardless of how
uncertain the prior is declared to be. \newline
\indent The risk-aversion coefficient $\delta$ has more impact: $\delta = 1$ produces a
materially worse Sharpe (0.07) compared to $\delta \in \{2, 3, 5\}$, which all yield
approximately 0.26. The degradation at $\delta = 1$ reflects how a lower risk-aversion
coefficient produces smaller equilibrium returns ($\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \delta
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{w}_m$), altering the posterior magnitude even when views
dominate its direction. For $\delta \geq 2$, the model is robust to further increases
in risk aversion. \newline
\indent The practical implication is direct: in this implementation, BL behaves like MVO
with momentum expected returns regardless of $\tau$. A practitioner wishing to
genuinely exploit the Bayesian blending mechanism would need views smaller in magnitude
relative to the equilibrium prior, or a substantially larger $\tau$ to reduce the
prior's pull. Tuning $\tau$ within the conventional range offers no benefit here. \\

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Black-Litterman returns and Sharpe ratio is entirely flat across $\tau \in \{0.01, 0.25\}$, confirming momentum views dominate the prior.
  \item Performance is inversely ordered by complexity: more inputs consistently produced worse results.
  \item Simplicity should be the rational default; complexity is justified only when input quality is high.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\noindent\textbf{\emph{More information, worse performance}} \\
\indent Perhaps the sharpest practical lesson of this study is that performance is
inversely ordered by informational complexity. Equal Weight consumes no inputs beyond
the asset universe itself. Risk Parity requires only the covariance matrix. MVO adds a
vector of expected returns. Black-Litterman adds an equilibrium prior and investor views. \newline 
\indent Yet the performance ranking runs in the opposite direction, with Equal Weight
in first place. Each additional layer of input estimation
introduces additional estimation error, and in a finite-sample setting that error
compounds faster than any informational benefit accrues. \newline 
\indent This does not mean complex
models are without value, but it means their value is conditional on the quality of their
inputs. Practitioners should treat simplicity as the rational default and reserve
complexity for settings where reliable return forecasts are genuinely available.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{keypoints*}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Results are specific to 18 UK assets over 2015--2025 and may not generalise to other settings.
  \item Performance is reported gross of transaction costs, which disproportionately disadvantages MVO.
  \item The asset universe is subject to survivorship bias due to ex-post selection of FTSE 100 constituents.
\end{itemize}
\end{keypoints*}

\indent \indent Several limitations of this study should be borne in mind when
interpreting its findings. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Single universe and time period}} \\
\indent All results are drawn from a single 18-asset UK multi-asset portfolio over
2015-2025. This period is characterised by a prolonged equity bull market and a
single rate-hiking episode: conditions that systematically favour Equal Weight's
equity-heavy allocation and structurally disadvantage an unlevered Risk Parity
strategy. \newline 
\indent A different sample period or asset universe could produce materially
different rankings. The finding that 1/N outperforms optimised strategies is
consistent with the broader literature \citep{demiguel_optimal_2009}, but the
magnitude of the gap is specific to this setting and should not be extrapolated. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{No transaction costs}} \\
\indent All performance metrics are reported gross of transaction costs. This
overstates net performance for all strategies, but the distortion is highly
uneven: MVO's average monthly turnover of 34.7\% means that transaction costs
would erode its returns far more severely than Equal Weight's 2.5\%. \newline 
\indent A realistic comparison would model costs explicitly. For example, as a fixed
basis-point charge applied to each trade at every rebalancing date. The relative
ordering of strategies could change once costs are accounted for, reinforcing
the case against MVO even further. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Fixed estimation window}} \\
\indent The 252-day rolling window used to estimate covariances and expected
returns is a fixed design choice that is not optimised or validated. Shorter
windows are more responsive to recent market conditions but introduce greater
estimation noise; longer windows are more stable but slow to adapt to regime
changes. \newline 
\indent The sensitivity of results to window length is not explored here and
represents a natural robustness check for future work. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Mechanical Black-Litterman views}} \\
\indent The momentum signal used to construct BL views is a systematic,
rules-based proxy rather than genuine investor views. The Black-Litterman
framework was designed to incorporate analyst forecasts, macroeconomic
judgments, and qualitative insights that a practitioner believes contain
information beyond what the market has already priced in. \newline 
\indent Using a mechanical momentum rule means BL is never operating as 
originally intended: it is effectively MVO with a pre-specified return signal, 
and its underperformance should be interpreted accordingly. \newline 
\indent Results could differ substantially with views constructed from fundamentals, 
macro factors or genuine active research. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Survivorship bias}} \\
\indent The 18 assets were selected ex-post as known, continuously-traded
constituents of the FTSE 100 over the sample period. \newline
\indent Companies that were delisted, went bankrupt, or fell out of the index 
during 2015-2025 are excluded by construction, which upward-biases the equity 
returns in the universe and understates the true risk of holding UK large-cap 
equities. \newline
\indent A more rigorous analysis would construct the universe using point-in-time 
index membership data.

\section{Conclusion}

\indent \indent This study implemented and compared four portfolio allocation strategies
across an 18-asset UK multi-asset universe over 2015-2025: an equal-weight benchmark,
Markowitz mean-variance optimisation, Black-Litterman and Risk Parity. The central
question was whether model sophistication translates into superior out-of-sample
performance. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{On the three research questions}} \\
\indent The first question, whether optimisation models outperform 1/N out-of-sample,
is answered clearly in the negative. Equal Weight delivered the highest total return
(93.9\%), the highest Sharpe ratio (0.36), and the highest Sortino ratio (0.47) of
all four strategies, outperforming Black-Litterman by over 31 percentage points in
cumulative return. \newline
\indent In answer to the second question, no optimised strategy improved
on Equal Weight on a risk-adjusted basis; the ranking by Sharpe is EW (0.36),
BL (0.26), RP (0.19), MVO (0.15). Model complexity consistently hurt rather than
helped. \newline 
\indent In answer to the third question, regime analysis reveals that no strategy
provides universal protection: BL excelled during the Brexit shock (+20.3\%), MVO was
uniquely resilient during the COVID-19 crash (+3.6\%) due to its concentration in safe
havens and Risk Parity, despite its theoretical all-weather appeal, was among the
worst performers in the 2022 rate-hiking episode ($-4.3\%$) when the equity-bond
correlation that underpins it broke down. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{The central finding}} \\
\indent The most striking pattern is that performance is inversely ordered by
informational complexity: the strategy consuming the fewest inputs performed best,
and each additional layer of estimation made things worse. \newline
\indent This is not a failure of portfolio theory. It is a consequence of estimation 
error in finite samples: optimisers amplify input noise into unstable, concentrated portfolios that underperform
simpler alternatives out-of-sample. \newline 
\indent Black-Litterman partially addresses this through
its equilibrium prior, but in this implementation the prior is dominated by mechanical
momentum views, reducing BL to MVO with a different signal. \newline 
\indent Risk Parity avoids expected return estimation entirely and provides 
meaningful downside protection, but its structural bond overweight drags 
performance in equity-dominated environments. \\

\noindent\textbf{\emph{Future work}} \\
\indent Several natural extensions follow from the limitations of this study. \newline 
\indent Modelling transaction costs explicitly would provide a more realistic comparison, 
particularly for MVO. \newline 
\indent Constructing the asset universe from point-in-time index membership data
would eliminate survivorship bias. Replacing mechanical momentum views with
fundamentals-based or macro-driven signals would allow BL to operate as intended. \newline
\indent Applying leverage to Risk Parity would bring it closer to its institutional
implementation. \newline 
\indent Finally, extending the comparison to include Hierarchical Risk Parity
and constrained MVO would complete the picture of how regularisation and structural
constraints affect the estimation error problem.
